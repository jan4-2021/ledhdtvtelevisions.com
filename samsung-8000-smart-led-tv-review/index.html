Samsung 8000 Smart LED TV Review

May 6th, 2011 by admin

With its super slim and stylish design the Samsung 8000 is one of the most aesthetically attractive TVs on the market. Itâs 55 inch display is bordered by a slim metallic bevel. The display measures in at a super-thin 30mm in thickness and is supported by an attractive chrome stand. All this is very nice, but more importantly how does the Samsung 8000 Smart LED TV measure up when it come to technical abilities?

Firstly 3D itâs capabilities. For the investment required to own one these TVs, you get a state of the art 55 inch LED 3D display. 3D TV has seen large growth in popularity in the last 12 months and all the major manufactures include this ability in their ranges of televisions. In the last six months of 2010, ten percent of all TVs sold were 3D capable. This year that figure is set to rise to thirty percent, and then up to fourty percent by 2013. If you had any doubts about the purchase of a 3D TV because of its sustainability, then rest assured that the electronics industry is well and truly committed to 3D home viewing. 3D televisions are well on the way to becoming a standard feature in home viewing.

If you are unconvinced about the effectiveness of 3D TVs, then it is likely itâs because youâve either not viewed a 3D TV for yourself, or youâve not viewed it on a TV that makes as good job of it as the Samsung 8000 3D TV does. If this is the case then you need to get yourself down to a televisions showroom and take a look at the Samsung 8000 LED TV. This TV is a great example of the potential of home 3D TV. That said it should be better than most of the models out there when considering it has a premium price tag attached to it. The combination of the 55â display and the LED lighting is the perfect platform for 3D content. The colors produced are vivid and given real depth by the 3D glasses, this TV more than holds its own against the competitors in its class. The ability to convert 2D content to 3D is a bit of a gimmick, it does add a slight new dimension to the pictures but when compared to native 3D movies or broadcasts it seems slightly ineffective. An added bonus from Samsung is that these TVs now come with 2 pairs of the active shutter glasses required to view in 3D (previously only one pair was included).

If there is a problem with the images displayed by this TV it is that there is a small amount of edge light bleeding. What is meant by this is that the LEDs which are placed round the edge of the LCD panel and are to light the LCD display, cause a whitening around the viewable area, especially when dark images are displayed. The negative effect can be made worse by not having the contrast and brighness settings configured well, but even when done right the bleeding cannot be totally eliminated.

Connectivity options are everything you would expect of a TV in this price bracket. It has more connectivity options than you will ever need to use and it has in-built analogue and digital receivers. The TV also has ports that are more associated with computers than TVs. This includes USB which amongst other things can be used to connect an external hard disk to use as a video recorder. Also the TV has and LAN port and optional wi-fi adaptor for connecting the TV to computers and the internet. This opens up a whole new world of possible content that can be displayed on the TV. Not only can you view your media from a PC, but the TV can be directly connected to the internet. This allows the viewer to watch on-demand content from the web, including movies, music and televisions series. Also you can surf the internet or data and news feeds can be displayed in a window at the same time as watching regular TV.

Lastly if you are a user of mobile smart devices such as the Galaxy SII or Tab, the Samsung 8000 can be connected to them using wifi and a feature called Samsung Smart Hub. This gives the user the ability to view content from the TV in another room of the house whilst another viewer watches something else on the TV. Now how many arguments will this prevent when it comes to who has control over the remote!
